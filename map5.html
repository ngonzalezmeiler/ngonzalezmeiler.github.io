Project Overview

This project focused on analysing the topography and historical development of a section of the River Lek in the Netherlands using Digital Elevation Model (DEM) data. The primary objective was to investigate the landscape characteristics of the river corridor by creating both a Digital Terrain Model (DTM) and a Digital Surface Model (DSM).
Additionally, historical cartographic data was incorporated to compare the river's past and present course, allowing for an assessment of how the river and its surrounding landscape have changed over time.

Methods

The project began with acquiring the DEM data covering the selected section of the River Lek. This elevation dataset was processed within a GIS environment to generate both a Digital Terrain Model (DTM) and a Digital Surface Model (DSM).
The DTM was used to represent the bare-earth surface by removing vegetation, buildings, and other surface objects, providing a clear view of the underlying terrain. In contrast, the DSM included all surface features, allowing for the analysis of landscape elements such as vegetation, infrastructure, and settlements alongside the river.
Both models were visualised using elevation symbology and hillshade techniques to enhance terrain interpretation. The DTM and DSM outputs were then compared to identify differences between the natural terrain and the contemporary surface environment.
To investigate historical changes, a georeferenced historical map of the River Lek was imported into the GIS project and aligned with modern spatial data. The historical map was then compared with the current river course and surrounding landscape features. This enabled the identification of changes in river morphology, floodplain structure, land use, and human modifications over time.

Reflection

This project provided valuable experience in terrain analysis, raster data processing, and historical GIS techniques. Creating both a DTM and DSM improved my understanding of the differences between terrain and surface models and demonstrated how each can be used to reveal different characteristics of a landscape.
One of the key challenges was working with historical maps, particularly ensuring accurate georeferencing so that meaningful comparisons could be made between historical and contemporary datasets. This process required careful selection of control points and highlighted the importance of spatial accuracy in GIS analysis.
Comparing historical and modern representations of the River Lek offered insights into how river systems evolve naturally and how human activities influence their development. The project demonstrated the usefulness of GIS for integrating multiple data sources to study environmental change over time.
Overall, the project strengthened my technical skills in QGIS, raster analysis, and spatial data management while also enhancing my ability to interpret geographic and historical patterns. It showed how combining elevation data with historical cartography can provide a deeper understanding of landscape evolution and river dynamics.
